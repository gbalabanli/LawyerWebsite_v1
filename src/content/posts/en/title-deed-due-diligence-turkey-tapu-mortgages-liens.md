For a foreign buyer, title deed due diligence is the legal backbone of a Turkish property purchase. The property may be attractive, the price may seem fair, and the seller may be ready to sign. But if the title deed record has a problem, the transaction can become expensive and difficult to unwind.

In Turkey, real estate ownership is based on land registry records. A buyer should therefore check the official record before signing a binding contract or sending a significant payment.

## Match the Property to the Record

The first question is simple: is the property being sold the same property shown in the title deed record? The answer is not always obvious. Foreign buyers should compare city, district, neighborhood, block, parcel, independent section number, floor, share ratio, and property type.

For apartments, the independent section details matter. A buyer may think they are buying a specific flat, but the record may show a different unit, storage area, or share. For land, zoning and development status should be checked separately because a land parcel is not the same as a buildable residential project.

Photographs, sales brochures, and agency listings are not official title records. They are useful for orientation, but the legal analysis must start from the registry.

## Identify Encumbrances

Encumbrances can change the risk profile of the transaction. A mortgage may mean the property secures a debt. A lien may show enforcement proceedings. An annotation may reflect a promise of sale, lease right, family residence issue, court dispute, or another restriction.

Not every encumbrance makes a sale impossible, but every encumbrance must be understood. If the seller promises to clear a mortgage, the contract should say how and when. If a lien exists, the buyer should not assume it will disappear automatically. If an annotation affects use or transfer, the buyer should understand its practical effect before closing.

Foreign buyers should also consider whether the property can be used for the intended purpose. A unit marketed as residential, commercial, or investment property should match legal and practical use expectations.

## Confirm Seller Authority

The registered owner may sell personally, through a company representative, through heirs, or through an attorney-in-fact. Each situation requires authority review.

If a company sells the property, signing authority, corporate approvals, and registry documents may be needed. If heirs are involved, inheritance documents and share authority should be checked. If a power of attorney is used, the wording should clearly authorize sale of the relevant property and receipt of payment if applicable.

Foreign buyers should be cautious when the person negotiating is not the registered owner. Agents can introduce and coordinate, but ownership transfer authority must come from the legal owner or a properly authorized representative.

## Document the Result Before Payment

Due diligence should produce a practical result: proceed, proceed only after conditions are met, renegotiate, or walk away. The purchase contract should reflect that result. If a mortgage must be cleared, make it a condition. If a title detail must be corrected, define the deadline. If a power of attorney is needed, approve the wording before signing.

Keep copies of title documents, registry checks, contract drafts, payment proof, and seller communications. If a dispute arises later, these records may become essential evidence.

Good due diligence does not guarantee that a transaction will never have problems. It does reduce avoidable risk and gives the buyer a clearer legal position before committing funds.
