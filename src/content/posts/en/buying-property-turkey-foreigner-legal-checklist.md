Buying property in Turkey can be attractive for foreign buyers, but the legal process should be handled carefully. A property may look ready for sale, the seller may appear cooperative, and the agent may say the transaction is standard. Even then, the buyer should not transfer money or sign broad documents before legal checks are complete.

The official rule is practical but strict: ownership is acquired through registration at the land registry. Preliminary contracts, reservation forms, notarized promises, or agency documents may create contractual rights, but they do not by themselves transfer ownership. This distinction is critical for foreign buyers.

## Verify the Seller and Title Deed

The title deed is the starting point. Confirm the registered owner, property type, location, plot and block details, shares, and whether the seller has full authority to transfer. If a company, representative, heir, or attorney-in-fact is involved, authority documents should be checked carefully.

Encumbrances matter. Mortgages, liens, annotations, usufruct rights, family residence annotations, litigation notes, or other restrictions can affect transfer, use, or resale. A buyer should know about these before making a binding payment.

Foreign buyers should also check whether the property is in a restricted area or subject to special limitations. Eligibility can depend on nationality, location, military or security zones, and land acquisition limits.

## Review the Contract Before Payment

Many disputes begin with a short reservation form or a one-sided preliminary contract. The contract should clearly state the property, parties, price, currency, payment schedule, delivery date, title deed appointment, default consequences, refund rules, and documents each side must provide.

If the seller promises furniture, renovation, rental income, citizenship eligibility, or delivery by a certain date, those promises should be written clearly. Marketing language is not the same as a binding contractual obligation.

Do not rely only on a translated summary. Foreign buyers should review the controlling language and understand which version prevails if the Turkish and English texts differ.

## Control the Payment Trail

Payment proof is important for ownership disputes, tax review, banking compliance, and citizenship files. Use traceable bank transfers where possible. Payment explanations should match the property and transaction. Keep receipts, bank slips, exchange records, and seller acknowledgments.

Avoid sending money to unrelated individuals unless there is a documented reason. If the seller, developer, agent, or attorney requests payment to a different account, the buyer should ask why and document the authority.

Currency conversion should also be planned. If the transaction may later be used for citizenship or residence purposes, payment evidence should be consistent and complete from the beginning.

## Check Closing and After-Sale Steps

Before the land registry appointment, confirm required documents, tax numbers, passport translation, valuation or appraisal requirements if relevant, compulsory earthquake insurance, and representative authority. If the buyer cannot attend, the power of attorney should be specific and reviewed before execution.

After closing, the buyer may need utility transfers, tax payments, property management arrangements, residence permit planning, rental contract review, or citizenship process coordination. These steps are easier when they are planned before transfer, not after a dispute appears.

The safest property purchase is not the fastest one. It is the one where the buyer understands the title, contract, payment trail, authority documents, and closing process before money changes hands.
