Foreign companies doing business with Turkish customers, distributors, suppliers, or partners may face unpaid invoices and delayed payments. Debt collection in Turkey should begin with evidence review. The best route depends on the contract, invoice records, delivery proof, debtor identity, dispute history, and whether the debtor is likely to object.

This guide explains how foreign companies can prepare a Turkish debt collection file before deciding between demand, enforcement, litigation, or settlement.

## Audit the Receivable File

Start by collecting the full receivable file. Useful documents include the contract, purchase orders, invoices, delivery records, customs or shipment documents, service acceptance records, correspondence, payment promises, account statements, and prior notices.

The debtor should be identified correctly. A trade name, brand name, website, or contact person may not be enough. The legal company name, tax number, trade registry details, address, and signatory information should be checked.

Foreign companies should also review governing law and jurisdiction clauses. A contract may point to foreign courts or arbitration, while enforcement against assets may still require action in Turkey. The dispute route should be chosen with enforcement in mind.

## Send a Structured Demand

A structured demand letter can clarify the debt and create pressure before formal proceedings. It should identify the creditor, debtor, contract, invoices, amount, currency, due date, interest claim, payment deadline, and consequences of non-payment.

The tone should be professional and evidence-based. A demand that is too vague may be ignored. A demand that overstates claims may weaken later settlement. Attachments should be organized so the debtor understands that the file is ready for legal action.

In some cases, a notarized notice or formal letter may be useful. The best format depends on the contract, debtor relationship, urgency, and evidence strategy.

## Choose Enforcement or Litigation

Debt collection in Turkey may involve enforcement proceedings, lawsuits, interim measures, or settlement. If the debt is well documented and the debtor has no serious defense, enforcement may create pressure quickly. If the debtor is likely to object, the creditor should be ready to prove the claim.

Litigation may be needed where the debtor disputes delivery, quality, authority, set-off, price, currency, or contract performance. In commercial matters, procedural prerequisites may also need review before court action.

The creditor should compare speed, cost, evidence strength, debtor assets, and business relationship. A fast filing is not useful if the evidence is disorganized or the debtor has no recoverable assets.

## Manage Objection and Settlement

Debtors may object to payment orders or raise factual defenses. The creditor should be prepared with documents showing contract formation, performance, delivery, invoice issuance, acceptance, and non-payment.

Settlement can be practical if it is secured correctly. Installment plans should include clear due dates, default consequences, interest, costs, and where possible, security. A weak settlement may only delay collection.

Foreign companies should also consider language and authority issues. Settlement documents should be signed by authorized persons, and translations should be handled carefully where needed.

## Preserve Commercial Leverage

Debt collection is not only a legal process. It is also a commercial decision. The creditor may want fast payment, continued business, asset pressure, public dispute avoidance, or a clean litigation record. The strategy should match that goal.

Before acting, review whether the debtor owns assets, continues operations, has other creditors, or is likely to hide assets. If asset risk is high, timing becomes more important.

For foreign companies, an organized Turkish collection file helps local counsel move faster and reduces translation, evidence, and authority problems later.
