Buying off-plan property in Turkey can be attractive for foreign buyers because prices may be lower before completion and payment plans may look flexible. The legal risk is that the buyer pays before receiving a completed, registered, and usable property. That creates exposure to construction delay, developer financial problems, unclear title transfer terms, zoning issues, and weak refund rights.

This guide explains what foreign buyers should review before paying a deposit or signing an off-plan property contract in Turkey.

## Check the Land and Developer

Before reviewing brochures, floor plans, or sales promises, check who legally owns the land and who has authority to sell the promised unit. The developer, marketing company, landowner, and sales agent may not be the same legal person. A foreign buyer should know which party will sign the contract and which party has the duty to deliver the property.

Title deed records should be checked for ownership, mortgages, liens, annotations, easements, and other restrictions. If the land has a mortgage or construction financing structure, the buyer should understand whether the future title deed can be transferred free of unexpected burdens.

The buyer should also review project permits, zoning status, construction license, unit allocation, and whether the promised independent section can legally exist as described. A beautiful sales presentation does not replace official records.

## Review the Contract Before Payment

The off-plan sales contract should identify the parties, project, unit, floor, size, payment schedule, delivery date, title transfer timing, penalties, defect procedure, cancellation rights, and dispute route. If the unit is described vaguely, later disagreement becomes easier.

Delivery date clauses deserve special attention. Some contracts give the developer broad extension rights for reasons that are not clearly defined. Others promise delivery but create weak penalties for delay. A buyer should know what happens if the project is delivered late, delivered incomplete, or not delivered at all.

Refund and termination clauses are equally important. If the buyer cancels, what happens to paid amounts? If the developer defaults, can the buyer recover payments? Are deductions allowed? Is interest payable? Does the contract require mediation, arbitration, or court proceedings?

## Control Payment Evidence

Payment evidence can become decisive in a dispute. Foreign buyers should avoid informal cash payments and should use traceable bank transfers where possible. Each payment should clearly refer to the buyer, seller, project, unit, and contract.

Receipts should be kept with the contract and payment schedule. If the buyer pays through an agent or third party, the authority of that person should be verified. A payment to the wrong party may create a separate dispute.

For citizenship or residence-related property planning, payment trail issues can be even more sensitive. The buyer should not assume that any payment method will support a later administrative application.

## Plan Delivery and Title Transfer

Delivery and title transfer are not the same thing. A buyer may receive keys before the title deed transfer, or may obtain title before the building is fully usable. The contract should explain what must happen before delivery and when the buyer receives the tapu.

Defect procedures should also be clear. The buyer should know how to inspect the unit, how to record defects, when the developer must repair them, and whether withholding part of the payment is possible. Without a written inspection procedure, practical pressure often falls on the buyer.

If the buyer will not be in Turkey at delivery, a power of attorney may be needed. The scope of that power should be carefully drafted so it allows necessary title and delivery steps without giving unnecessary authority.

## Watch for Common Red Flags

Red flags include pressure to pay before contract review, vague seller identity, promises that conflict with official records, unclear title transfer dates, broad developer delay rights, missing penalty clauses, cash payment requests, and refusal to provide project documents.

Another red flag is a contract that treats all marketing promises as non-binding. If sea view, size, finishing quality, parking, social facilities, or delivery date are important, they should be reflected in enforceable documents.

Foreign buyers should also be careful with currency clauses. The payment currency, exchange rate, late payment interest, taxes, fees, and title deed expenses should be clear before signing.

## Why Legal Due Diligence Matters

The purpose of legal due diligence is not to make every purchase complicated. It is to identify whether the buyer is paying for a legally deliverable property under enforceable terms. In off-plan transactions, that review should happen before deposit payment.

Once a dispute begins, the buyer's position depends heavily on the documents already signed and the payments already made. A clear contract, clean title review, organized payment evidence, and realistic delivery protections can prevent expensive conflict later.
