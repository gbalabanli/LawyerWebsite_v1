Esports image rights and streaming rights are often more valuable than they first appear. A player's name, nickname, face, voice, stream channel, clips, social media content, and match footage can all have commercial value. If those rights are drafted too broadly, a player may lose control over their personal brand. If they are drafted too narrowly, a team or sponsor may not be able to run normal campaigns.

This checklist explains the rights that should be reviewed in esports player contracts, sponsorship agreements, streamer contracts, and team content deals.

## Define Image Rights Clearly

Image rights should not be a single vague sentence. The contract should identify what is covered: legal name, nickname, gamertag, photo, voice, signature, biography, jersey image, team photo, match footage, interview footage, social media posts, stream clips, and other likeness elements.

The agreement should state who can use those rights, for what purpose, on which platforms, in which territory, and for how long. A team may need rights for roster announcements, match promotion, sponsor posts, website profiles, tournament media, and historical archives. A sponsor may need rights for a campaign, but not unlimited rights forever.

Players should check whether the contract allows paid advertising using their image. Paid ads can reach audiences far beyond the original esports community and may place the player next to products they did not expect to endorse.

## Separate Streaming Rights from Team Content

Streaming rights should be handled separately from general image rights. A player's personal stream may include subscription revenue, donations, ads, affiliate links, sponsor overlays, product placements, and platform bonuses.

The contract should explain whether the player can stream independently, whether team practice or scrims can be streamed, whether streaming hours are required, whether the team can control schedule or platform, and whether team sponsors must appear on personal streams.

Revenue should be clear. If the player owns the channel, the contract should not accidentally transfer channel income to the team. If the team creates a team channel or shared content series, revenue sharing should be written separately.

## Control Sponsor and Brand Use

Sponsor obligations can affect image and streaming rights. A player may be asked to wear branded apparel, place sponsor logos on stream, mention products, join campaigns, post affiliate links, or appear in advertisements.

The agreement should define approval rights and content limits. A player should know whether the sponsor can edit clips, create new ads, reuse old content, combine the player's image with other influencers, or keep campaign content online after the sponsorship ends.

Exclusivity should also be reviewed. A sponsor may restrict competing brands, but the category should be specific. Broad restrictions can limit a player's future income from personal streams or brand deals.

## Set Rules for Post-Contract Use

One of the most common issues is what happens after the contract ends. Can the team keep the player's profile on its website? Can the sponsor keep using an ad? Can old match footage stay online? Can the player ask for posts to be deleted?

The answer should be written. Many contracts allow archive or historical use, but restrict new advertising after termination. This can be a fair balance: the team can preserve history, while the player is not used in new campaigns without consent.

If a player transfers to another team, post-contract rights become more sensitive. Old sponsor content may conflict with the new team's sponsor. The contract should address this risk before transfer.

## Protect IP and Personal Brand

Players and streamers may own logos, overlays, emotes, catchphrases, music, artwork, or character-style branding. The contract should not transfer those assets unless the player clearly intends that result.

Teams and sponsors should also protect their own marks. Player content should not misuse sponsor logos, team names, tournament marks, publisher assets, or third-party music.

The best esports contracts treat image rights and streaming rights as separate commercial assets. They should be specific enough to support normal promotion, but limited enough to protect the player's long-term brand.
