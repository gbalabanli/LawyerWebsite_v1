Power of Attorney in Turkey for Foreign Property Buyers

June 20, 2026 Av. Barış C. Balabanlı
real-estate-foreignersforeign-buyersproperty-law

Contents

  • Define the Transaction Purpose
  • Limit the Authority
  • Check Execution Formalities
  • Keep Payment Control Separate

A power of attorney can make a Turkish property purchase much easier for a foreign buyer who cannot travel for every step. It can allow a trusted representative to sign documents, attend the land registry, complete title transfer, or handle post-closing steps. But it can also create serious risk if the wording is too broad.

Foreign buyers should treat a power of attorney as a legal instrument, not as a standard form. The representative’s authority should match the buyer’s actual need and the specific transaction.

Define the Transaction Purpose

Start by listing what the representative must do. Does the buyer need someone to sign only the title deed transfer? Review municipal records? Sign a preliminary contract? Obtain tax numbers? Connect utilities? Apply for insurance? Manage a citizenship restriction?

Each task may require different wording. A power of attorney that is too narrow may be rejected by an authority. A power of attorney that is too broad may expose the buyer to unnecessary risk. The goal is functional precision.

If the property is already identified, include clear property details where appropriate. If the buyer is still searching, the authority may need to be structured differently.

Limit the Authority

Broad powers can be dangerous. Some documents give authority to buy, sell, mortgage, lease, collect money, open accounts, sign settlement documents, or appoint others. A foreign buyer should not grant these powers unless they are necessary and understood.

For a purchase, the representative may need authority to complete land registry steps, sign required forms, submit documents, and receive the title deed. That does not always mean the representative should have authority to sell the property later or receive large payments.

Substitution authority should also be reviewed. If the representative can appoint someone else, the buyer should understand who may actually act in the transaction.

Check Execution Formalities

A power of attorney may be issued in Turkey before a notary, at a Turkish consulate abroad, or through a foreign notary route with apostille and translation where applicable. The correct method depends on the country, language, document type, and authority that will use the document.

Passport details must be accurate. Name spelling should match the passport and other transaction documents. If the document is prepared in two languages, the controlling text should be clear.

Foreign buyers should also check whether photographs, tax numbers, sworn translation, or additional certifications are needed for the intended use.

Keep Payment Control Separate

The safest approach is often to keep payment under the buyer’s direct control. If a representative must make payments, the authority and banking process should be documented carefully. The buyer should know where funds are going and why.

Avoid sending purchase money to an agent or representative without written authority and a clear contract structure. If a seller asks for payment to a third party, the reason should be documented and reviewed.

A good power of attorney helps the transaction move without giving away more control than necessary. Before signing, foreign buyers should understand exactly who can act, what they can sign, what property is involved, and whether money can move under the document.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a foreign buyer purchase property in Turkey by power of attorney?

Yes, a properly prepared power of attorney can allow a representative to complete transaction steps, but the authority should be limited and reviewed.

Should payment authority be included?

Only if necessary and clearly controlled. Broad payment or bank authority can create avoidable financial risk.

Can a power of attorney be issued abroad?

It may be issued through a Turkish consulate or foreign notary process with required authentication and translation, depending on the country and document.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Define the Transaction Purpose

    Identify whether the representative will inspect documents, sign contracts, attend land registry, receive title, or handle utilities.

  2. Limit the Authority

    Avoid broad wording where a narrow property purchase authority is enough.

  3. Check Execution Formalities

    Confirm notarization, consular process, apostille, sworn translation, and passport details before use.

  4. Keep Payment Control Separate

    Use traceable buyer-controlled payments unless a specific and reviewed authority is required.
